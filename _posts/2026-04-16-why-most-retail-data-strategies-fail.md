Most retail organisations I meet have a data strategy. Slide decks, roadmaps, platform diagrams. Some have invested millions in cloud infrastructure. And yet, the business still runs on Excel.

The problem is rarely the technology. It's everything around it.

## The three failure patterns I see most often

**1. Starting with the platform, not the problem**

The first instinct is often to modernise the data stack. Migrate to the cloud, implement a lakehouse, standardise ingestion. These are all valid moves — but they consume 18 months of budget and goodwill before a single business user sees any value.

The data team ships infrastructure. The business ships another season without better decisions. Trust erodes.

Start with a use case that matters to the CFO or the Head of Buying. Something with a clear before/after. Build the platform *around* it, not before it.

**2. Treating data as an IT project**

Data strategy is a business strategy. When it lives in IT, it gets prioritised like IT — which means it waits behind system upgrades and licence renewals.

The most effective retail data organisations I've seen have a direct line from the data function to the CEO or CCO. Not because the technology is complex, but because the decisions it enables are strategic.

Margin, inventory, customer lifetime value — these are board-level topics. Your data function should be in that conversation.

**3. Underestimating the change management**

You can build a perfect demand forecasting model and have buyers ignore it because it threatens their autonomy. You can deploy a real-time store dashboard and have district managers dismiss it because they didn't ask for it.

Data changes how decisions get made. That's uncomfortable. The organisations that succeed spend as much energy on adoption as on development — often more.

## What actually works

The retail organisations making real progress share a few things:

- They pick **one or two use cases** with measurable ROI and go deep before going wide
- They have **a senior sponsor** who uses the output themselves
- They treat **data literacy** as a core capability, not a one-time training
- They build **incrementally**, shipping something useful every quarter

The irony is that the most successful data strategies often look boring. No fancy architecture diagrams. Just clear problems, focused teams, and relentless follow-through.
